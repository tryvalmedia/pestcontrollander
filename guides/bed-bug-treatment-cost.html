Heat vs. chemical: the honest comparison

Heat treatment

Industrial heaters raise room to 130–140°F for 6–8 hours, killing all life stages including eggs in one visit. No insecticide residue. More expensive upfront but typically resolves infestation in a single appointment. Best for: whole-room or whole-home infestations, fast resolution, pet/child sensitivity to chemicals.

Chemical treatment

Residual insecticides, dusts, and bed bug monitors applied to mattress, bed frame, baseboards, and furniture. Requires 2–3 visits spaced 10–14 days apart to address eggs as they hatch. Lower upfront cost but longer treatment timeline. Best for: budget-conscious situations, apartments where heat treatment isn't practical.

Why bed bugs cost so much more than other pests

Three factors drive bed bug treatment costs above all other pest treatments:

  1. Eggs resist chemical treatment — Most insecticides don't kill bed bug eggs. Chemical protocols require multiple visits timed to when eggs hatch, extending the treatment over weeks.
  2. Hiding ability — Bed bugs can hide in electrical outlets, inside walls, behind baseboards, and in dozens of other locations that require systematic, thorough treatment of every item in a room.
  3. Labor intensity — A proper treatment requires moving and treating furniture, inspecting and treating every seam of every mattress, and carefully treating baseboards and crevices. This takes hours per room.

Red flags when hiring a bed bug exterminator

  • Guaranteeing complete elimination in one chemical visit — this isn't how it works
  • Refusing to provide a written treatment protocol before you agree to service
  • Not asking to inspect the extent of infestation before quoting
  • Quoting a flat price for the whole home without inspection first
  • Offering heat treatment at suspiciously low prices — legitimate heat treatment requires industrial equipment and trained operators
Act immediately — bed bugs spread fastA single female bed bug can lay 200–500 eggs in her lifetime. A minor early-stage infestation ($500–$1,000 to treat) can become a whole-home infestation ($3,000–$6,000) within 3–4 months if left untreated. Every week of delay makes the infestation worse and the treatment more expensive.

Frequently asked questions

How much does bed bug heat treatment cost?

Bed bug heat treatment costs $500–$800 per room and $2,000–$4,000+ for a whole-home treatment in 2026. Heat treatment typically requires one appointment of 6–8 hours and kills bed bugs in all life stages including eggs. It costs more than chemical treatment but is generally more effective and faster.

How many treatments does it take to get rid of bed bugs?

Chemical treatments typically require 2–3 visits spaced 10–14 days apart to address eggs as they hatch. Heat treatment can eliminate an infestation in a single visit by killing all life stages simultaneously. The number of treatments also depends on the severity of the infestation.

Can you sleep in your house after bed bug treatment?

For chemical treatment, most technicians recommend staying out of treated rooms for 4–8 hours until surfaces dry. You can typically sleep in the home that night. For heat treatment, you can return the same evening once the home has cooled down. Fumigation requires 2–3 nights away from the home.

Should I throw out my mattress if I have bed bugs?

Not necessarily. A licensed professional can treat a mattress in place with steam, insecticide, and a mattress encasement. Disposing of a mattress can actually spread bed bugs to other areas of your home. Discuss with your exterminator before discarding any furniture.
