How to Get Rid of Mice (Step-by-Step, 2026)

Trapping mice without sealing their entry points is like bailing water with the drain open. Here's the correct order of operations.

Updated April 2026  ·  PestControlQuotesNow.com

The correct sequenceStep 1: Seal entry points. Step 2: Set traps inside. Step 3: Remove food and water sources. In that order. Most DIY mouse treatments fail because people start with traps but skip exclusion — new mice enter as fast as you catch them. A single mouse family can produce 35–60 offspring per year.

Step 1: Find and seal every entry point

Mice can enter through holes as small as ¼ inch — roughly the size of a ballpoint pen. Common entry points:

  • Gaps where pipes enter walls (under sinks, behind appliances)
  • Where dryer vents or HVAC lines penetrate exterior walls
  • Foundation cracks and gaps around the sill plate
  • Gaps around utility boxes and electrical conduit
  • Weep holes in brick construction
  • Garage door seals that don't contact the floor

Materials for sealing: Steel wool packed into gaps (mice can't chew through it) plus caulk or foam to hold it in place. Hardware cloth (¼ inch mesh) for larger openings. Expanding foam alone is not effective — mice can chew through it.

Step 2: Set snap traps strategically

Snap traps (Victor, T-Rex) are the most effective and humane DIY mouse control tool. Glue boards are less effective and more cruel. Key placement rules:

  • Place traps perpendicular to walls with the trigger end facing the wall — mice run along walls and will trigger the trap as they pass
  • Use peanut butter as bait — it's more attractive than cheese and harder for mice to remove without triggering
  • Place traps every 2–3 feet along active runways (look for droppings to identify routes)
  • Set multiple traps at once — don't underestimate the population
  • Check and reset traps daily

Step 3: Eliminate food and water sources

  • Store all food (including pet food) in heavy plastic or metal containers with tight lids
  • Don't leave dishes in the sink overnight
  • Fix any leaking pipes or faucets
  • Keep compost bins away from the house
  • Reduce clutter in basements, garages, and attics — mice nest in undisturbed piles

Signs of active mouse infestation

  • Droppings (small, dark, rice-shaped pellets) along walls, behind appliances, in drawers
  • Gnaw marks on food packaging, wiring, wood, or soft materials
  • Nesting materials (shredded paper, fabric, insulation) in hidden areas
  • Musky odor in enclosed areas
  • Scratching sounds in walls or ceilings at night

Frequently asked questions

How do you know if you have mice in your walls?

Signs of mice in walls: scratching or scurrying sounds in walls at night, chewed wires or soft materials near wall penetrations, droppings along baseboards, and a musky odor in enclosed spaces. Mice are primarily nocturnal — activity you can't see but can hear at night is a strong indicator.

How long does it take to get rid of mice?

With proper exclusion + trapping, most infestations can be significantly reduced within 1–2 weeks. Complete elimination typically takes 2–4 weeks once all entry points are sealed. Without exclusion, mice will continue to enter regardless of how many traps you set.

Do mice come back after extermination?

They will if entry points aren't sealed. Mice follow pheromone trails left by previous inhabitants. New mice are attracted to the same entry points and nesting areas. Proper exclusion — sealing every gap ¼ inch or larger — is the only lasting solution.

Is mouse droppings dangerous?

Yes. Mouse droppings can carry Hantavirus (potentially fatal), Salmonella, and other pathogens. Do not sweep droppings dry — spray with disinfectant first, let soak for 5 minutes, then wipe with gloves and mask. Bag and dispose of materials contaminated with droppings or nesting material.
