Signs of Bed Bugs: How to Know for Sure

Bed bug bites look like many things. The bites alone aren't proof. Here's how to confirm whether you actually have bed bugs — and what to do if you do.

Updated April 2026  ·  PestControlQuotesNow.com

The key truth about bed bug bitesBite patterns alone cannot confirm bed bugs — many people have no reaction at all, while others react to dust mites, fleas, or mosquitoes with similar-looking marks. Confirmation requires finding physical evidence: live bugs, shed skins, fecal spots, or eggs on the mattress, bedding, or nearby furniture.

The 7 signs of bed bugs (ranked by reliability)

1. Live bed bugs (most reliable)

Adult bed bugs are visible to the naked eye — about ¼ inch long (apple seed size), flat and oval when unfed, reddish-brown and swollen after feeding. Check mattress seams and edges, the gap between mattress and box spring, and the frame joints of your bed. Use a flashlight and a credit card to probe seams. Bed bugs are nocturnal and hide in tight cracks during the day.

2. Fecal spots (dark brown or black dots)

Bed bugs leave behind dark fecal spots (digested blood) that look like ink dots from a fine-tip marker. Check mattress seams, the back of the headboard, box spring fabric, and wall outlet covers near the bed. If you touch a fresh fecal spot with a wet cotton swab, it will smear rusty-brown.

3. Shed skins (exuviae)

Bed bugs molt 5 times before reaching adulthood, leaving behind translucent, pale-yellow shell casings. Finding multiple shed skins is strong evidence of an established infestation — it means the bugs have been there long enough to go through several life stages.

4. Blood stains on bedding

Small rusty or reddish stains on sheets, pillowcases, or mattress fabric — caused by bed bugs being crushed while you sleep on them after feeding, or by residual blood from bite sites. These stains are different from menstrual stains in that they're small, rust-colored spots scattered near sleeping positions.

5. Eggs and eggshells

Bed bug eggs are tiny (1mm), pearl-white, and barrel-shaped. They're found in mattress seams, wood furniture joints, and wall crevices near the bed. Very difficult to spot without magnification, but a sign of a reproducing infestation.

6. Bites in a line or cluster

While bites alone can't confirm bed bugs, bite patterns can suggest them: small red welts in a line (often called "breakfast, lunch, dinner" rows) or a cluster of 3–5 bites, appearing on exposed skin (arms, neck, shoulders, legs). Not everyone reacts to bed bug bites — 30% of people have no visible reaction.

7. Musty odor in the bedroom

Large infestations produce a musty, sweet odor (sometimes compared to coriander or almonds) from the bugs' scent glands. This is rarely noticeable in early infestations and is only a reliable indicator in severe, established cases.

Where to check for bed bugs

  • Mattress seams and edges — use a credit card to expose and examine seams
  • Between mattress and box spring
  • Bed frame joints, screw holes, and crevices
  • Headboard (front and back)
  • Nightstand and dresser joints and drawers
  • Baseboards and carpet edge near the bed
  • Electrical outlet covers within 5 feet of the bed
  • Behind hanging pictures or loose wallpaper near the bed

What to do if you find signs of bed bugs

  1. Don't panic and don't move your mattress or furniture to another room — this spreads the infestation
  2. Document what you found — take photos of any evidence you find, including location
  3. Bag and wash all bedding immediately in hot water (130°F+) and dry on high heat for 30+ minutes — this kills all stages including eggs
  4. Call a licensed exterminator for an inspection — many offer free inspections and can confirm identification and recommend treatment method
  5. Don't sleep elsewhere in the house — this moves the infestation to wherever you relocate

Frequently asked questions

How do I know for sure if I have bed bugs?

The only way to confirm bed bugs is to find physical evidence: live bugs, shed skins (pale yellow casings), fecal spots (dark ink-like dots) on mattress seams, blood stains on bedding, or eggs in furniture joints. Bites alone cannot confirm bed bugs — other insects and skin conditions cause similar marks.

Can you see bed bugs with the naked eye?

Yes. Adult bed bugs are about ¼ inch long (apple seed size), flat, oval, and reddish-brown. They're visible without magnification but hide in tight cracks during the day. Eggs and nymphs are much smaller and harder to spot without a magnifying glass and flashlight.

Do bed bugs stay in just one room?

In early infestations, yes — they cluster near their host (your bed). As populations grow, they spread to nearby rooms, especially if you start sleeping elsewhere. This is why sleeping in another room when you suspect bed bugs is counterproductive — you're essentially leading them to a new location.

How quickly do bed bugs spread through a house?

In early stages, bed bugs are contained near sleeping areas. Without treatment, a small infestation can spread to adjacent rooms within 3–6 months. In apartments, they can spread through wall voids, plumbing chases, and hallways to neighboring units. Early detection and treatment prevents spread.
