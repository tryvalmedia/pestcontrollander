Liquid barrier vs. bait system: the key decision

Liquid soil barrier

Termiticide (usually fipronil/Termidor) injected into soil around foundation. Kills on contact; non-repellent formulas let termites carry poison back to colony. Faster knockdown. Lasts 5–10 years. Annual renewal: $100–$300. Better for active infestations.

Bait station system

Stations installed every 10–15 ft around perimeter. Termites feed on slow-acting bait, share with colony, causing collapse over weeks/months. Lower environmental impact. Requires annual monitoring contract ($300–$500/yr). Better for prevention and long-term monitoring.

For active infestations, most pest professionals recommend liquid Termidor — it's faster, more effective at immediate colony elimination, and the 10-year total cost (including annual renewals) is typically lower than a bait system with annual monitoring fees.

The cost of NOT treating

The average termite damage repair bill is $1,800, and it frequently climbs to $5,000–$10,000 for established colonies that have been feeding for 2–5 years. Termites can consume a foot of 2×4 lumber in under six months in warm climates. Treatment at $1,500 is always cheaper than structural repairs at $5,000–$20,000+.

Termite damage is NOT covered by standard homeowner's insuranceMost homeowner's policies explicitly exclude termite damage as "preventable pest damage." Annual termite monitoring and warranty coverage from your pest control company is effectively the insurance policy for this risk.

Signs you may need termite treatment

  • Mud tubes running along your foundation, walls, or crawl space joists
  • Hollow-sounding wood when tapped along baseboards or door frames
  • Discarded wings near windows or doors (post-swarm debris)
  • Frass (small pellet-like droppings) near wood surfaces — drywood termite indicator
  • Stuck or warped doors and windows — termite tunneling can distort wood frames

Frequently asked questions

How much does termite treatment cost?

Termite treatment costs $500–$3,000 for most homes in 2026. Liquid soil barriers run $1,000–$3,200. Bait station systems run $1,500–$3,000 plus $300–$500/year monitoring. Tent fumigation for drywood termites runs $1,200–$3,800. Cost depends on home size, termite species, severity, and treatment method.

What is the best termite treatment method?

For active subterranean termite infestations, liquid barrier treatment with Termidor (fipronil) is generally the most effective — it kills colonies faster and the 10-year total cost is lower than ongoing bait monitoring fees. Bait systems are preferred for prevention and properties where liquid treatment is impractical. Drywood termites typically require fumigation or heat treatment.

Does homeowner's insurance cover termite damage?

No. Standard homeowner's insurance policies explicitly exclude termite damage because it's considered preventable maintenance. Annual termite monitoring with a warranty from a licensed pest control company is the primary way to protect against this financial risk.

How long does termite treatment last?

Liquid termiticide barriers (Termidor/fipronil) typically provide 5–10 years of protection. Bait station systems provide protection as long as active monitoring contracts are maintained. Annual inspections are recommended regardless of treatment method to confirm the barrier or stations remain effective.
