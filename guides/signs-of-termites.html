10 Signs of Termites You Should Never Ignore

Termites work silently and rarely show themselves — but they leave unmistakable clues. Here's exactly what to look for and where.

Updated April 2026  ·  PestControlQuotesNow.com

The key problem with termitesUnlike ants or roaches, termites are almost never seen until an infestation is well-established. By the time visible damage appears, colonies have often been active for 2–5 years. Knowing what to look for — and where to look — is the only reliable way to catch them early, when treatment is significantly cheaper.

The 10 signs of termites

1. Mud tubes on your foundation

Subterranean termites (the most common species in the US) build pencil-width mud tubes made of soil, wood particles, and saliva to travel between their underground colony and the wood in your home. These tubes are found along your home's foundation, on concrete block walls, in crawl spaces, and along floor joists. Finding a mud tube — even an empty one — confirms subterranean termites are or have been active on your property.

2. Hollow-sounding wood

Tap along baseboards, door frames, and wood trim. A solid thud means healthy wood. A hollow or papery sound means termites have been eating the interior of the wood, leaving only a thin shell. Run a screwdriver along suspected areas — if it sinks in too easily, that's a strong sign of structural damage.

3. Discarded wings near windows and doors

Reproductive termites ("swarmers") shed their wings after mating, often in spring near light sources. Finding small, equal-sized wings (about ¼ inch long) in windowsills, on floors near doors, or in spider webs is a strong indicator of a nearby or established colony. This typically happens in spring for subterranean species.

4. Frass (termite droppings)

Drywood termites push their feces out of tiny holes in infested wood. The result is small piles of pellet-shaped, sawdust-colored droppings (frass) near wood furniture, baseboards, or walls. If you find frass, you have drywood termites — a different species requiring different treatment (usually fumigation).

5. Stuck or warped doors and windows

As termites tunnel through wood in door frames and window frames, they create moisture and distortion that makes doors and windows difficult to open or close. This is easily confused with normal settling or humidity changes, which is why it's often dismissed — but if it develops quickly in a dry season, termites are worth ruling out.

6. Bubbling or uneven paint

Termite activity beneath surfaces can cause paint to bubble, peel, or appear water-damaged in areas that have no water source. If you see this pattern along baseboards, wall surfaces, or near flooring in dry areas of your home, have it inspected.

7. Visible damage to wood structures

In advanced infestations, you may find wood that appears to have a honeycomb interior, wood that crumbles when touched, or structural members that look "eaten away" along the grain. This is severe damage indicating years of activity — and means significant repair costs in addition to treatment.

8. Tight-fitting floors and ceiling tiles

Termites cause moisture damage as they work. This can cause hardwood floors to blister or buckle in patterns that resemble water damage. Ceiling tiles may sag or discolor. If this occurs in areas without any water history, termites may be the cause.

9. Swarmer termites themselves

You may see winged termites (swarmers) themselves, especially in spring. They're often confused with flying ants — but termites have straight antennae, equal-length wings, and a straight body, while flying ants have bent antennae, unequal wings, and a pinched waist. Seeing swarmers inside your home is a serious sign of an established colony.

10. Unusual sounds from walls

In severe infestations, soldier termites bang their heads against wood and worker termites create a rustling sound inside walls. This is rarely heard in early infestations but can be noticed in quiet rooms at night with an established colony. A faint tapping sound from walls with no other explanation warrants inspection.

Where to check for termites

  • Along the foundation exterior and crawl space — check for mud tubes
  • Basement walls, especially where wood contacts concrete
  • Crawl space floor joists and subfloor
  • Around plumbing penetrations through concrete slabs
  • Wood porch steps, door frames, and window frames
  • Any wood in contact with soil (fence posts, wood mulch against foundation)
What to do if you find signs of termitesDo NOT disturb mud tubes or damage — this can cause the colony to relocate and make treatment harder. Get a professional inspection as soon as possible. Frequently asked questions

What do termites look like?

Worker termites are small (¼–⅜ inch), pale white or cream-colored, and rarely visible since they stay inside wood and soil. Swarmer termites are dark brown or black with wings. Soldier termites have larger, darker heads. Most homeowners never see the termites themselves — they identify infestations by the damage signs described above.

What is the difference between flying ants and termite swarmers?

Flying ants have bent (elbowed) antennae, two sets of unequal wings, and a pinched waist. Termite swarmers have straight antennae, two sets of equal-length wings, and a straight body without a pinched waist. Both may appear in spring, but finding termite swarmers inside your home indicates an established indoor colony.

Can you have termites without seeing mud tubes?

Yes. Drywood termites (common in the South and Southwest) live entirely within wood and never build mud tubes. Their presence is identified by frass (small pellet-like droppings) and hollow-sounding or damaged wood. If you're in an area with drywood termites, the absence of mud tubes doesn't rule out an infestation.

How long does a termite colony take to cause serious damage?

Subterranean termite colonies can cause significant structural damage within 3–8 years in warm climates. Drywood termites are slower, typically taking 5–10 years to cause serious structural damage. Formosan subterranean termites (Gulf Coast) are the most aggressive — they can cause severe damage in 2–4 years.
