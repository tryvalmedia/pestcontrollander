How to Get Rid of Ants: What Actually Works

Spraying ants you can see is the most common mistake. The only way to permanently eliminate ants is to reach the colony. Here's how.

Updated April 2026  ·  PestControlQuotesNow.com

The fundamental ruleKilling visible ants with spray does almost nothing — you're removing 1–5% of the colony. The queen and 95%+ of workers remain underground, continuing to reproduce. Permanent ant elimination requires either reaching the colony directly or using slow-acting bait that workers carry back to the queen.

What works: DIY options ranked

1. Slow-acting gel bait (most effective DIY)

Products like Terro (borax-based) or Advion (indoxacarb) work because they're slow-acting — workers carry the bait back to the colony and share it before dying. Place bait directly on ant trails without disturbing the trail. Don't spray anything near active bait. Allow 1–2 weeks for full colony effect. This works well for most house ant species.

2. Diatomaceous earth (perimeter/mechanical)

Food-grade DE damages the exoskeleton of ants crossing it. Apply to entry points, baseboards, and windowsills. Not a colony solution on its own — use as a supplement to bait. Effective at physical barriers but needs reapplication after moisture.

3. Perimeter spray (exterior barrier)

Residual insecticide sprays applied around the exterior foundation, windows, and doors create a contact-kill barrier. Bifenthrin and permethrin-based products (Bifen IT, Suspend SC) are effective for several weeks. Must be reapplied. This addresses foraging workers but doesn't reach the colony.

What does NOT work

  • Windex or dish soap — kills visible ants but leaves foraging pheromone trail intact
  • Cinnamon, coffee grounds, essential oils — repellents that may redirect the trail, not eliminate it
  • Contact-kill sprays on ant trails — actually makes infestations worse in some species (pharaoh ants) by triggering "budding" where the colony splits into multiple sub-colonies

When to call a professional exterminator for ants

DIY treatment works well for minor odorous house ant and pavement ant infestations. Call a professional if:

  • Carpenter ants — these nest inside moist or damaged wood and can cause structural damage. Requires finding and treating the nest.
  • Pharaoh ants — spraying pharaoh ants causes colony "budding" (splitting into multiple sub-colonies). Only targeted gel bait works, and it requires strategic placement throughout the structure.
  • Multiple entry points you can't seal — professional technicians can identify all entry points and treat the exterior perimeter more thoroughly than OTC products
  • Recurring infestations — if you've had the same ant problem 3+ seasons, DIY isn't solving the root cause

Frequently asked questions

Why do I keep getting ants in my house?

Recurring ant infestations usually mean the colony is nesting inside or very close to your home, and you're only eliminating foraging workers rather than the colony itself. Common root causes: moisture issues (ants seek water), food sources, wall voids providing indoor nesting sites, and gaps in the foundation or walls providing easy entry.

Does killing ants attract more ants?

Not directly. However, killing visible ants on a trail without destroying the colony leaves the pheromone trail intact — other workers will continue to follow the same path. This is why spray-and-wipe solutions provide temporary relief only.

How long does ant exterminator treatment last?

A professional one-time treatment for most ant species lasts 4–8 weeks. Recurring quarterly service maintains a perimeter barrier year-round and typically includes callbacks if ant activity resumes between visits.

Are carpenter ants dangerous?

Carpenter ants don't eat wood like termites — they excavate it to nest inside. They prefer moist or decayed wood. Left untreated, a mature carpenter ant colony can cause significant structural damage over 5–10 years. They're harder to eliminate than most ant species because nests are often deep inside walls or structural wood.
